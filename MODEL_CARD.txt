🧠 Model Card — Multimodal Artificial Intelligence for Head and Neck Small Round Blue Cell Neoplasms

1. Model Overview

This repository provides a multimodal artificial intelligence framework developed to support the diagnosis and prognostic stratification of head and neck small 
round blue cell neoplasms (SRBCNs).

The model was designed for four tumour groups:

| Class | Tumour Group                           |
| ----- | -------------------------------------- |
| 0     | Haematolymphoid                        |
| 1     | Mesenchymal                            |
| 2     | Neuroectodermal / Neural Crest-Derived |
| 3     | Melanocytic                            |

The computational framework integrates:

* Clinicopathological variables
* Nuclear morphometric descriptors
* H&E histopathological image patches
* Conventional machine learning using XGBoost
* SHAP-based explainability
* Multimodal convolutional neural networks
* CellViT++ cellular representation learning
* Foundation model pipelines using UNI and Virchow
* Conventional and machine learning-based survival analysis

The model is intended as a research-oriented decision-support framework for computational pathology.

This model is not intended for autonomous clinical diagnosis.

2. Study Design and Data

This study was designed as a multicentre retrospective investigation using anonymised histopathological and clinicopathological data.

A total of 675 cases were included:

| Tumour Group                           | Number of Cases | Percentage |
| -------------------------------------- | --------------- | ---------- |
| Haematolymphoid                        | 463             | 68.6%      |
| Neuroectodermal / Neural Crest-Derived | 84              | 12.4%      |
| Mesenchymal                            | 75              | 11.1%      |
| Melanocytic                            | 53              | 7.9%       |

The cohort included 362 male patients and 313 female patients. The mean age at diagnosis was 41.8 ± 27.8 years.

At the last follow-up, 435 patients were alive and 240 had died.

3. Input Data

The framework supports the following input modalities:

* H&E-stained whole-slide images
* Image patches extracted from annotated regions of interest
* Clinicopathological variables
* Nuclear morphometric features
* Survival data
* Foundation model embeddings

Core structured variables include:

* Age
* Sex
* Anatomical location
* Tumour group
* Diagnosis
* Nuclear area
* Nuclear perimeter
* Nuclear circularity
* Nuclear eccentricity
* Survival time
* Vital status

5. Nuclear Morphometric Analysis

Significant differences were observed across tumour groups for nuclear morphometric variables.

| Tumour Group                           | Nuclear Area | Perimeter | Circularity | Eccentricity |
| -------------------------------------- | ------------ | --------- | ----------- | ------------ |
| Haematolymphoid                        | 19.30        | 19.31     | 0.767       | 0.728        |
| Mesenchymal                            | 17.81        | 17.61     | 0.783       | 0.770        |
| Neuroectodermal / Neural Crest-Derived | 30.40        | 30.06     | 0.733       | 0.751        |
| Melanocytic                            | 23.73        | 23.36     | 0.745       | 0.795        |

Neuroectodermal/neural crest-derived tumours showed the largest nuclei, while melanocytic tumours demonstrated the highest eccentricity.

9. Calibration

Calibration improved with increasing model complexity.

| Model     | ECE   | Brier Score |
| --------- | ----- | ----------- |
| AlexNet   | 0.093 | 0.214       |
| ResNet50  | 0.036 | 0.104       |
| CellViT++ | 0.024 | 0.082       |

CellViT++ produced the most reliable probability estimates among the evaluated multimodal architectures.

10. Explainability

SHAP analysis was used to interpret the XGBoost classifier and survival model.

The most influential diagnostic features were:

* Age
* Nuclear perimeter
* Nuclear eccentricity
* Nuclear area

Age was the dominant predictor for haematolymphoid, mesenchymal, and neuroectodermal/neural crest-derived tumours.

Nuclear perimeter was the dominant predictor for melanocytic tumours.

Sex and most anatomical location variables showed minimal influence on model output.

11. Survival Analysis

Conventional survival analysis included:

* Kaplan–Meier analysis
* Log-rank testing
* Univariable Cox regression
* Multivariable Cox regression

Kaplan–Meier analysis demonstrated significant differences in overall survival according to:

* Tumour group
* Diagnosis
* Anatomical location
* Nuclear area
* Nuclear perimeter
* Nuclear circularity
* Nuclear eccentricity

No significant survival differences were observed according to age or sex.

In multivariable Cox regression, nuclear area remained independently associated with overall survival:

| Variable     | HR   | 95% CI    | p-value |
| ------------ | ---- | --------- | ------- |
| Nuclear area | 1.02 | 1.00–1.03 | 0.012   |

13. Intended Use

This model is intended for:

* Computational pathology research
* Evaluation of multimodal AI approaches
* SRBCN classification research
* Nuclear morphometric analysis
* Foundation model benchmarking
* Survival prediction research
* Development of diagnostic and prognostic decision-support systems

This model is not intended for:

* Autonomous clinical diagnosis
* Replacement of expert pathologist review
* Direct clinical deployment without further validation
* Use on unvalidated populations, institutions, scanners, or staining protocols

14. Limitations

Key limitations include:

* Retrospective study design
* Multicentre variability in staining and scanning
* Potential institutional bias
* Uneven tumour group distribution
* No public release of raw whole-slide images
* Dependence on tissue quality and ROI selection
* Need for further validation in larger independent cohorts
* Model outputs require expert pathological interpretation

15. Potential Biases

Potential sources of bias include:

* Institutional differences in tissue processing
* Scanner-specific image characteristics
* Staining variability
* Geographic variation in case distribution
* Uneven class prevalence
* Retrospective archival case selection
* ROI-based sampling bias

Centre-aware splitting and external validation were used to reduce data leakage and assess generalisability.

16. Ethical and Responsible AI Considerations

All data were anonymised and used under institutional ethical approvals and data-sharing agreements.

Due to patient privacy and institutional restrictions:

* Whole-slide images are not publicly distributed
* Raw clinicopathological data are not publicly shared
* Patient-identifiable metadata are not included in this repository

The framework is intended for research and decision-support development only.

Predictions must always be interpreted by qualified pathologists in conjunction with morphology, immunohistochemistry, molecular findings, and clinical context.
